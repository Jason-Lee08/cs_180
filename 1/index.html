Single-Scale Approach:

In my implementation for smaller images, I first crop the image to remove the noise that image borders introduce by only using the inner 90% of the image (so I crop 5% of the outer width/height on each side). This also improves my program's speed by lowering the number of pixels to iterate over. To align the images, I find the offsets of the red/green channels relative to the blue channel. To determine the optimal x/y offset, I iterate over potential offsets in the table shown below and find the x/y offsets that maximize the normalized cross-correlation of the differences in raw images and the images with canny edge detection (Ïƒ=0.9).

Multi-Scale Approach:

To support larger ".tif" images, I first scale the input image down by half until its width is less than 700 pixels. I chose 700 pixels because it gave the best balance between finding the best offsets while minimizing compute time. The algorithm starts by calling the single-scale approach function and passing the smallest image first with a very large potential offset range. Then, I repeat this process in an image pyramid, scaling up the image by two until the image is scaled to its original resolution, passing the doubled previous offset and searching +/- 2 offset at each step.
